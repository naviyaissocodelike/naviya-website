# DC/SF Thesis Research Brief

## The DC Thesis: Government Drives AI Innovation That Matters

**Core claim:** Proximity to government produces disproportionate AI innovation in the areas that matter most (security, infrastructure, institutional adoption), and DC is the best place to be close to that.

### Evidence For

**The money is real and accelerating fast.** Federal AI contract value jumped from $670M obligated in 2024 to $7.2B in 2026, a 966% increase. The DoD requested $13.4B for AI and autonomous systems as a standalone budget line in FY2026. Total potential contract value hit $91.8B, up 1,912% from 2024. This isn't speculative. These are committed federal dollars.

**The use cases are expanding.** Federal agencies reported 3,600+ AI use cases in 2025, up 69% year-over-year and 5x from 2023. Nearly 90% of federal agencies are using or planning to use AI. This is moving from experimentation to operational deployment.

**Defense tech is having its moment.** Defense tech startups raised $49.1B in 2025, nearly double 2024's $27.2B. The sector was validated by real-world deployment in Ukraine, and mainstream VC firms reframed defense investing as "supporting democratic values." Anduril hit $30.5B valuation. Shield AI raised at $2.8B. This capital is concentrated in DC-adjacent companies.

**DC's ecosystem is underrated.** Ranked 12th globally for startups, ahead of everywhere in the US except SF, NYC, and Boston. Tech workforce grew 7.9% in 2025 with 15,000+ companies across AI, cloud, and quantum. The DC-to-NYC corridor holds 30% of all AI startups. Companies like Palantir and Vannevar are already building here specifically for government proximity.

**Cybersecurity is an AI multiplier.** Cyber VC approached $150B total invested in 2025. AI-powered security, zero-trust architecture, and critical infrastructure protection are the largest investment categories. DC is the natural gravity well for this because the buyers (federal agencies, defense, intelligence) are here.

### Evidence Against

**Procurement is genuinely broken.** Federal procurement rules are aligned with pre-digital capital projects. Promising startups abandon government work because timelines don't match private funding cycles. Risk-averse culture prioritizes compliance over experimentation. Technologies get stuck in pilot programs for years.

**The talent gap is real.** SF has 35% of all US AI engineers. DC has a fraction of that. The Mid-Atlantic had only 8.5% of national AI job postings in early 2025. The most ambitious AI engineers still default to SF. DC attracts policy people and defense-oriented builders, but not the frontier ML researchers.

**Government innovation is derivative, not generative.** The government doesn't build the foundational models. It buys and deploys them. The real AI innovation (the architectures, the training runs, the breakthroughs) happens in SF labs. DC companies are integration and deployment layers on top of frontier models built elsewhere. "Government gets more innovation that's used in AI" might be true for applied AI, but the generative core stays in SF.

**DOGE and budget uncertainty.** The current political environment introduces real volatility into federal tech spending. Budget negotiations, agency restructuring, and shifting priorities mean that the massive spending numbers could get redirected or cut.

### Blind Spots to Watch

**The "valley of death" between contract and product.** Many DC AI companies optimize for winning contracts, not building great products. This creates a class of companies that are lucrative but not actually innovative. Being close to them doesn't build the kind of expertise that transfers.

**Regulatory capture risk.** Proximity to government can make you an apologist for slow institutional adoption rather than a critic of it. The thesis is about redesigning institutions, not just serving them.

**The classified wall.** A huge portion of the most interesting government AI work is classified. You can be in DC and still have zero visibility into the applications that matter most unless you have clearance.

**AI governance is still mostly talk.** There's a lot of activity around AI policy in DC, but very little of it has produced binding, consequential regulation. Being in the room for policy discussions is valuable for network, but the actual governance infrastructure is still nascent.

## The SF Thesis: AI x Crypto Is the Next Infrastructure Layer

### What's Happening

**The convergence is real, even in the trough.** 27% of crypto companies are now building AI products, up from 14% in 2022. 40% of all crypto VC in 2025 went to AI-integrated projects, up from 18% the prior year. Crypto VCs describe the market as "in the trough" as of early 2026, which is typically when the best investments get made.

**Decentralized compute is finding product-market fit.** Render Network evolved from artist rendering to powering AI inference at scale. Akash became the primary host for uncensored LLMs banned from AWS/Azure. io.net aggregates GPUs into virtual clusters rivaling centralized providers. These aren't theoretical. They're running production workloads.

**The agentic economy is crypto-native.** Active autonomous AI agent deployments surpassed 20,000 across blockchain networks by February 2026, up 300% from Q4 2025. Agents need to transact, hold value, and operate autonomously. Crypto rails are the natural infrastructure for this.

**Institutional capital is arriving.** Grayscale and Bitwise filed for spot ETFs covering Bittensor's TAO token. The AI crypto market cap hit $20.9B. NVIDIA's projection of $1T in chip demand through 2027 sent AI tokens surging. This is moving from speculative retail trading to institutional infrastructure.

**SF is still the center.** SF and NYC account for 37% of global blockchain funding. Stanford and Berkeley produce the highest density of AI PhDs. The overlap of AI researchers and crypto builders in SF is unique globally.

### What to Optimize for in Each City

**In DC, optimize for:**
- Relationships with defense tech founders and operators (the Anduril/Shield AI/Vannevar network)
- Understanding government AI procurement and deployment firsthand
- Policy community access (but be a participant, not a spectator)
- Deal flow in govtech, cybersecurity, and defense AI
- Content and visibility around AI governance and institutional adoption

**In SF, optimize for:**
- AI x crypto deal flow (decentralized compute, agentic infrastructure, tokenized AI)
- Relationships with frontier AI researchers and crypto protocol teams
- Understanding what's real vs. hype in the AI x crypto intersection
- DA's West Coast presence and angel network
- Deep technical fluency in the infrastructure being built

### The Synthesis

The DC thesis is strongest as a claim about **applied AI at institutional scale**: the government is the world's largest buyer, it's spending aggressively, and proximity to that creates real opportunity. It's weakest as a claim about **frontier innovation**: the generative core of AI stays in SF.

The SF thesis is strongest as a claim about **the next infrastructure layer**: AI x crypto is building the financial and computational rails that the agentic economy will run on. It's weakest as a claim about **near-term returns**: the trough is real, and most AI x crypto projects will fail.

The dual-city strategy works because each thesis covers the other's weakness. DC gives institutional credibility and defense/govtech deal flow. SF gives frontier exposure and crypto/AI infrastructure access. The combination is rare and valuable.
